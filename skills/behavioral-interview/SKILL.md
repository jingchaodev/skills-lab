# Behavioral Interviewing

Behavioral interviews predict future performance from past behavior: *tell me
about a time you actually did X*, not *what would you do if X*. This skill
covers the competency areas to probe, the STAR method for extracting real
signal, how to distinguish rehearsed answers from genuine ones, level
calibration, and debrief writing — for both interviewer and candidate.

Many companies organize these around a named principle framework (leadership
principles, company values, competency models). The framework names differ; the
underlying competencies and the interviewing craft are the same. This skill
teaches the generic skill.

## The Competency Areas

Prepare 2-3 questions per competency you're assigned. Common areas and sample
questions:

| Competency | What it tests | Sample question |
|------------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Customer focus** | Works backwards from user needs | "Tell me about a time you went above and beyond for a user." |
| **Ownership** | Takes responsibility beyond the assigned scope; long-term thinking | "Tell me about a problem no one owned that you stepped in on." |
| **Dealing with ambiguity** | Makes progress without complete information | "Tell me about a decision you made with incomplete data." |
| **Bias for action** | Moves fast on reversible decisions; calculated risk | "Tell me about a time you took a calculated risk to seize an opportunity." |
| **Delivering results** | Ships with the right quality under constraints | "Tell me about a project you delivered under significant pressure." |
| **Insisting on high standards** | Won't accept "good enough"; fixes root causes | "Tell me about a time you raised the quality bar when others thought it was fine." |
| **Conflict / backbone** | Challenges respectfully, then commits | "Tell me about a time you strongly disagreed with your manager." |
| **Earning trust** | Candid, admits mistakes, follows through | "Tell me about difficult feedback you gave a peer." |
| **Diving deep** | Stays connected to detail; skeptical when metrics and anecdotes differ | "Tell me about a significant problem you found by digging into the details." |
| **Invent & simplify** | Novel solutions; removes complexity | "Tell me about a complex problem you solved simply." |
| **Learning / curiosity** | Seeks knowledge outside their comfort zone | "Tell me about something hard you had to learn to solve a problem." |
| **Developing others** (mgmt) | Raises the bar with hires; grows people | "Walk me through a promotion case you built" / "how you managed out an underperformer." |

Emphasis shifts by role: an engineer's *ownership* is technical-debt and
operational ownership; a manager's is org-level; a program lead's is
cross-team delivery. Weave customer focus into every answer regardless of role.

## STAR: What to Listen For

STAR is the shape of a complete answer. Listen for all four; probe for whatever's
missing.

- **Situation** — specific context: when, where, what team, what scale.
- **Task** — what *their* responsibility was (not the team's).
- **Action** — what *they specifically* did. Listen for "I," not "we."
- **Result** — quantified outcome: metric, business impact, timeline.

At the start of a behavioral slot, tell the candidate you'll use STAR, that
you'll probe further, and that you'll be taking notes. Then ask one question at
a time, give them a moment to think, and only probe for what they haven't
already volunteered.

## Probing for Real Signal

Rehearsed answers stay at the "we" and narrative level. Probes pull the
candidate down to their specific contribution and decisions.

**Depth probes** (what did YOU do?):
- "What was your specific contribution versus the team's?"
- "What data did you use to make that decision?"
- "What alternatives did you consider, and why this one?"
- "Walk me through your decision-making step by step."

**Authenticity probes** (is this real?):
- "What would you do differently if you could redo it?"
- "What was the hardest part?"
- "Who disagreed with you, and why?"

**Scale probes** (for senior roles):
- "How many people/teams were involved?"
- "What was the business impact in dollars/users/metrics?"
- "What was the blast radius if it had failed?"

**Results probes:**
- "You mentioned revenue — what percentage change year over year?"
- "What tradeoffs did you make — quality, cost, time?"
- "How did the result compare to your actual goal?"

If a story is too old, too team-level, or too perfect, ask for a different one:
"Can you give me a more recent example?" or "What's a story where it didn't go
well?"

## Red Flags vs Green Flags

| Red flags | Green flags |
|-----------|-------------|
| Uses "we" exclusively; can't isolate their contribution | Clear "I drove…" with specific actions |
| No metrics or quantified impact | Quantified results (latency, revenue, users, team size) |
| Story is too perfect — no mistakes, tradeoffs, or disagreement | Acknowledges mistakes and what they learned |
| Can't explain *why* they made a decision | Shows judgment in tradeoffs, not just effort |
| Blames others for failures | Owns their part of a failure |
| Story complexity is below the target level | Complexity matches the target level |
| Hypothetical answers ("I would…") instead of real experience | Names specific people, decisions, and outcomes |
| Has to be guided toward their own solution | Structures and drives their own thinking |
| "Plans to build metrics" = no data-driven approach today | Data-driven frameworks (ROI, impact assessment) in use now |

A subtle one: **sparse notes that are just lists of system/team names, with no
description of what each does, usually mean the candidate gave vague answers** —
not that the slot didn't probe. The brevity is itself the signal.

## Level Calibration

Rate the *scope, ambiguity, complexity, influence, and impact* of the story
against the target level — not how likeable the candidate is.

| Signal | Junior | Mid / senior | Staff+ |
|--------|--------|--------------|--------|
| Scope | A feature or component | Team-level architecture | Org-level strategy |
| Ambiguity | Clear requirements | Defined problem, unclear solution | Undefined problem *and* solution |
| Complexity | Straightforward-to-hard | Multiple constraints | Conflicting constraints |
| Influence | Own team | Related teams | Whole organization |
| Impact | Feature quality | Team product | Org operations |

For management roles, strong senior signals include: independently defines
strategy, forms technical opinions, authors decision docs, escalates when
needed, drives outcomes the team wouldn't reach without them. Weak signals
(ceiling): purely process-oriented (tracks boards), reactive rather than
anticipatory, relies on others for all decisions, no authored artifacts.

## Interview Structure (60-min slot)

- **Intro (0-10 min).** Introduce yourself and the format. Explain STAR, that
  you'll probe, and that you'll take notes. If it's a technical/design slot,
  frame it: "Drive the conversation, ask clarifying questions, think out loud."
- **Questions (10-55 min).** 2-3 questions per assigned competency. One at a
  time; probe only for missing info; keep going until you can confidently rate.
  Take detailed notes verbatim where you can.
- **Candidate questions + wrap (55-60 min).** Let them ask. Don't answer things
  you're unsure of — refer to the hiring manager or recruiter. Explain next
  steps.

**Live-interview prep doc tips:** order the doc by question sequence (not by
topic) so you run it top to bottom; co-locate the calibration rubric and a
strong-answer sketch directly under each question so you grade in place; use
real clock times ("2:45-2:55 Intro"); and clearly mark any material that is
interviewer-only and must not be read aloud.

## Debrief & Feedback Writing

Write feedback that another person could act on without having been in the room.
Structure per story:

1. **Competency assessed** — which principle(s) the story maps to.
2. **Story summary** — 2-3 sentences of what happened.
3. **Evidence** — the specific behaviors that demonstrate (or fail to
   demonstrate) the competency.
4. **Level calibration** — why this is junior/senior/staff scope.
5. **Concerns** — gaps, red flags, weak evidence.
6. **Vote** — inclined / not inclined, with justification.

Rating language: consistently-positive = **strength**; mostly positive = **mild
strength**; mixed = **mixed**; mostly negative = **mild concern**; consistently
negative = **concern**.

**Common debrief mistakes:** voting on gut feel without citing evidence;
conflating likeability with competency; holding candidates to a higher bar than
current team members; not distinguishing "didn't demonstrate" from "demonstrated
poorly"; anchoring on one strong/weak story and ignoring the rest.

## Coaching a Candidate

Candidates need **8-10 strong stories** that collectively cover the competencies,
each mapping to 2-3 of them. Story selection criteria:

- Recent (last 2-3 years), significant scope, clear personal contribution.
- Quantified results.
- Contains a real challenge or conflict — not "everything went well."
- You can speak to it for 10-15 minutes with depth.

For each story, map: primary competency, 1-2 secondary competencies, the level
signal (what makes it senior scope), the conflict, the learning, and the
metrics. Pitfalls: too many shallow stories, stories over 3 years old, stories
where they were a passenger, no failure stories (reads as low self-awareness),
and no articulated "so what."

## Note on Internal Transfers

If a candidate is an internal transfer rather than an external hire, the format
often differs: shorter, more conversational, anchored on their real recent work
rather than abstract problems, and evaluated on ramp speed and fit rather than
pass/fail signals. Confirm which format applies *before* preparing questions.

## Tips

- **Past behavior over hypotheticals.** "Tell me about a time" beats "what would
  you do." A hypothetical answer to a behavioral question is itself a flag.
- **Probe until you can rate, then stop.** Don't burn time once the signal is
  clear.
- **"We" is a prompt to probe, not a verdict** — pull them to "I."
- **Grade scope, not polish.** A rehearsed, articulate story can still be below
  level; a rough story can be strong signal.
- **Cite evidence in every debrief line.** "Gut feel" is not feedback.
- **A story with no mistakes is a yellow flag** — it usually means the candidate
  isn't telling you the hard part.
