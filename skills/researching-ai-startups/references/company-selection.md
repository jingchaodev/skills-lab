# Company Selection

Use this mode when the user names a category, market, theme, or desired company list rather than one company.

## 1. Set the scope

Record:

- research question;
- category and adjacent categories;
- time frame and geography when relevant;
- target company count;
- user interests that affect relevance;
- output format and destination.

If the user gives no company count, start with 8–12 companies. Prefer a useful comparison over a long directory.

## 2. Build a discovery map

Search across independent buckets:

1. established category leaders;
2. newer entrants;
3. infrastructure and developer-tool companies;
4. application companies where relevant;
5. open-source or incumbent alternatives;
6. companies or evidence that challenge the dominant category story.

Do not select companies only from fundraising lists, accelerator pages, or one investor portfolio.

## 3. Explain every company in plain language

For each company, answer when evidence exists:

| Field | Question |
|---|---|
| Company | What is its official name and website? |
| Category | What market or technical category is it in? |
| Product | What does it sell, in one concrete sentence? |
| User and buyer | Who uses it and who pays? |
| Founders | Who founded it and what background is relevant? |
| Founded and funding | What dates and financing are reliably sourced? |
| Technical advantage | What appears difficult or different technically? |
| Business model | How does it appear to make money? |
| Business progress | What customer, revenue, usage, or deployment evidence is verified? |
| Hiring signals | Which capabilities is it building now? |
| Useful interviews | Which founder or technical sources deserve deeper reading? |
| Evidence gaps | What important facts remain unclear? |

Do not force a value into every field. Write `Unknown` with a short reason instead of guessing.

## 4. Order deeper research

Order companies using:

1. relevance to the requested category;
2. product clarity;
3. technical distinctiveness;
4. source quality and availability;
5. useful founder or technical interviews;
6. hiring relevance;
7. independent evidence.

This order means **worth deeper research**, not **good employer**. Explain the reason in one sentence per company. Funding and media attention may help discovery but are not quality scores.

## 5. Source and contradiction checks

For every company selected for the final table:

- include at least one official source;
- look for one independent or customer-side source when available;
- identify whether customer claims describe a pilot, partnership, logo, or production use;
- record missing or conflicting evidence;
- avoid copying category labels without explaining the underlying product.

## 6. Output

Start from `templates/company-selection.md`. Group sources by company and state what each source supports. Keep the comparison compact enough that rows remain comparable.

## Stop when

- the requested company count is reached;
- the list includes more than one discovery bucket;
- each included company has a plain product explanation and founder check;
- the research order is based on relevance and evidence rather than hype;
- important gaps are named;
- another search round yields mostly duplicates.
