# Single-Company Research

Use this mode when the user names one company or selects one from a company comparison.

## Start with a coverage map

Create this table before searching:

| Area | State | Best current source | Missing evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Company overview | Not started | — | — |
| Founders | Not started | — | — |
| Product and technology | Not started | — | — |
| Business model | Not started | — | — |
| Culture | Not started | — | — |
| Careers | Not started | — | — |

Allowed states are `Not started`, `Sources found`, `Note complete`, and `Evidence gap`. A keyword in a source does not make an area complete.

## Company overview

Answer:

- What problem does the company solve?
- Who experiences the problem and who pays?
- What old workflow or product does it replace or improve?
- Why is the product possible or important now?
- What does the company claim, what can be observed, and what is independently supported?

Preferred sources: product documentation, product demonstrations, customer evidence, and credible reporting. Avoid rewriting the homepage as analysis.

## Founders

Research:

- names, roles, education, and relevant work history;
- prior history working together;
- why they chose the problem;
- division of product, engineering, research, and sales responsibility;
- principles repeated across multiple public statements;
- relevant past failures, disputes, or contradictions when credibly sourced.

Preferred sources: founder profiles, long-form interviews, prior employer records, conference biographies, and reliable reporting. Do not turn this into a celebrity biography or infer character from prestige.

## Product and technology

Explain:

- the product workflow from the user’s point of view;
- important system components and boundaries;
- what the company builds versus buys;
- where models, data, evaluation, infrastructure, and human review fit;
- the main technical constraints;
- claimed differences and competing alternatives;
- evidence that the product works in production.

Preferred sources: technical documentation, engineering posts, demos, papers, talks, and detailed customer material. A benchmark chosen by the company is attributed evidence, not an independent comparison.

## Business model

Cover:

- buyer and sales motion;
- public pricing or contract structure;
- deployment and integration burden;
- customer concentration and platform dependence;
- verified business progress;
- the difference between partnerships, pilots, contracts, and recurring production use.

Do not infer revenue from funding, logos, headcount, or contract headlines.

## Culture

Examine:

- decision-making;
- engineering and product ownership;
- speed versus quality;
- customer proximity;
- hiring bar;
- location expectations;
- how conflict, mistakes, and failed work are handled;
- whether public values have concrete operating examples.

Preferred sources: multiple founder interviews, employee writing, operating documents, detailed job descriptions, and credible independent accounts. A careers-page values list alone is not evidence of actual culture.

## Careers

Record:

- current role families;
- repeated technical requirements;
- seniority distribution;
- location expectations;
- signs of which systems are being built now;
- roles relevant to the user’s stated interests.

Treat job descriptions as signals about current needs, not a complete architecture map. Describe the hiring surface without recommending that the user apply.

## Finish the collection

1. Update each coverage state only after its note exists.
2. Keep raw sources separate from edited notes.
3. List conflicting evidence and unresolved questions in the index.
4. Keep discovered interviews in a backlog until they receive substantive analysis.
5. Stop when the requested areas are covered or remaining gaps cannot be resolved from public evidence.
