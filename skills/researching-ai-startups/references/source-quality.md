# Source Quality

Apply these rules in both research modes.

## Source order

Prefer sources in this order:

1. official product and technical documentation;
2. official company and founder writing;
3. complete founder and technical-leader interviews;
4. product demonstrations and conference talks;
5. detailed customer evidence;
6. career pages and job descriptions;
7. reliable independent reporting;
8. critical or conflicting sources;
9. secondary summaries when primary material is unavailable.

Primary does not mean unbiased. It means close to the event or claim. Search for independent support and contradiction separately.

## Evidence labels

| Label | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Direct | Visible in documentation, product behavior, a primary record, or reproducible data |
| Attributed | Said directly by a named founder, employee, customer, or other source |
| Independent | Supported by a credible source outside the company |
| Inferred | Reasoned from multiple facts but not directly confirmed |
| Unverified | A lead or claim that has not been confirmed |

Labels describe support conditions, not truth. An attributed founder statement may be accurate, incomplete, or promotional.

## Required source record

Record, when available:

- exact title;
- author, speaker, or organization;
- publisher;
- publication date;
- access date;
- canonical URL;
- source type;
- transcript provenance when relevant;
- which claim or research area it supports;
- important limitations.

Do not turn relative dates such as “two years ago” into exact dates without confirming them.

## Claim handling

For every important claim:

1. identify who made it;
2. state whether it is direct, attributed, independent, inferred, or unverified;
3. preserve numbers, time periods, and qualifications such as `up to`, `pilot`, or `planned`;
4. seek a second source for disputed or decision-relevant claims;
5. record a contradiction instead of silently choosing the preferred story;
6. mark time-sensitive facts with a date.

## Common mistakes

- Customer logo ≠ verified production adoption.
- Partnership announcement ≠ recurring revenue.
- Funding ≠ product demand.
- Founder philosophy ≠ actual culture.
- Benchmark claim ≠ independent technical advantage.
- Search snippet ≠ complete source.
- Multiple pages repeating one press release ≠ independent confirmation.
- Missing public evidence ≠ proof that the underlying fact is false.

## Confidence and gaps

Use plain explanations instead of false precision. State why confidence is high, medium, or low, then list the missing source that would change it. Stop searching after two or three refinement rounds produce mostly duplicates; report the gap rather than filling it with speculation.
