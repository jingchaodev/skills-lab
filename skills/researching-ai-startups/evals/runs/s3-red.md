# Decagon: employer research baseline

## What the product concretely is

Decagon sells an enterprise platform for building, operating, and improving customer-facing AI agents. Its agents work across chat, voice, and email, while “Agent Operating Procedures” let teams specify workflows in natural language rather than a proprietary configuration language. The product includes tools for building agents, evaluating and optimizing their behavior, and deploying them across channels. [Decagon product site](https://decagon.ai/)

This is not merely a customer-support chatbot: the company positions the agent as an “AI concierge” spanning the customer lifecycle. That broader framing is Decagon’s marketing language, not independently demonstrated product scope. [Decagon product site](https://decagon.ai/)

The current hiring taxonomy provides a more concrete view of the underlying system: Decagon lists engineering roles in agent orchestration, agents, cloud infrastructure, core infrastructure, data infrastructure, developer experience, enterprise product, research, voice/speech, and security. [Decagon careers](https://decagon.ai/careers)

## Claims audit

### One company claim

Decagon displays a customer testimonial attributed to Janelle Sallenave, identified as a COO, claiming **70% chat-and-voice resolution**. The homepage does not expose enough context to determine the customer, measurement window, definition of “resolution,” escalation policy, or whether Decagon independently audited the metric. Treat it as a vendor-published customer claim, not a comparable benchmark. [Decagon homepage](https://decagon.ai/)

### One independently supported or unresolved claim

Decagon markets itself as a unified platform for building, optimizing, and scaling agents across voice, chat, and email. Its hiring footprint independently supports the existence of substantial orchestration, infrastructure, data, research, voice, and enterprise-product work. However, public materials reviewed here do **not** resolve how much of the stack is proprietary versus orchestration around third-party foundation models, how much model training occurs internally, or how production reliability compares with competitors. [Decagon product site](https://decagon.ai/) · [Decagon careers](https://decagon.ai/careers)

## Culture claim

Co-founder Ashwin Sreenivas describes a speed-oriented culture involving trade-offs made to preserve velocity; the interview explicitly covers six-day in-office expectations, shipping in roughly two days, delaying hiring until approximately $1 million in ARR, and plans to scale headcount rapidly. These are founder-reported operating expectations, not an independent employee-experience sample. [Knuckle Up interview and chapter list](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_FmwqPzqkjY)

The careers page advertises flexible PTO, free meals, company events, healthcare, competitive compensation, and other benefits, but it does not publish engineering on-call expectations, typical weekly hours, attrition, promotion criteria, manager quality, or remote-work flexibility. [Decagon careers](https://decagon.ai/careers)

## Explicit unknowns

- ARR, net retention, gross margin, customer concentration, contract duration, and inference economics.
- Independently measured containment, hallucination, latency, and production-incident rates.
- Proprietary-model scope, training data rights, evaluation methodology, and model-vendor dependence.
- Engineering attrition, on-call load, working hours, promotion calibration, and whether six-day attendance applies uniformly.
- How much infrastructure work is reusable agent-platform engineering versus customer-specific integration.
- Equity ranges, dilution, exercise windows, and current internal valuation.
- Reporting lines and technical ownership for orchestration, memory/context, evaluation, and post-deployment learning roles.
